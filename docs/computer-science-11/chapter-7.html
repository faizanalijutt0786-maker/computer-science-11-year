Chapter 7: Legal and Ethical Aspects of Computing

Student Learning Outcomes​

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

  • Understand the fundamental legal and ethical considerations in computing systems
  • Identify various privacy and security threats in digital environments
  • Explain strategies for preventing and mitigating security threats
  • Discuss the digital divide and its implications for different social groups
  • Recognize and address bias in computing systems
  • Utilize information safely and responsibly
  • Assess the impact of computing on individuals and society
  • Apply principles of digital citizenship and ethical conduct

Introduction​

In today's digital world, computing systems play a crucial role in every aspect of our lives — from personal devices to complex networks. As these systems become more integrated into our daily routines, understanding the legal and ethical considerations associated with their use is essential.

This chapter delves into various aspects of digital technology, including terms of use, privacy and security threats, and the digital divide. The legal and ethical challenges in computing cover issues such as data protection, intellectual property rights, and compliance with relevant laws. Ethical considerations address privacy, fairness, and the broader societal impacts of technology.

7.1 Understanding Terms of Use​

Terms of Use (also called Terms and Conditions or Terms of Service) are legal agreements that outline how a service or product can be used. These terms are set by the provider (like a website, app, or software company) and must be agreed upon by the user. The main purpose is to establish a fair and transparent relationship between the provider and user, ensuring both parties understand their rights and obligations.

Example: When you use a Pakistani online shopping platform like Daraz, the Terms of Use may include clauses about payment methods, return policies, and delivery procedures.

7.1.1 Importance​

Understanding Terms of Use is important for several reasons:

  1. Protection of Rights — Terms of Use protect users by defining their rights, such as the right to privacy, the right to receive certain service levels, and the right to seek redress if something goes wrong.

    Example: Food delivery services like Foodpanda specify what happens if an order is incorrect or the delivery is delayed.

  2. Clarity and Transparency — These terms explain what the service provider will deliver, under what conditions, and what actions are not permitted. This prevents misunderstandings and helps users make informed decisions.

    Example: Mobile payment apps outline transaction limits, fees, and processes for reporting unauthorized transactions.

  3. Legal Safeguards — For businesses, Terms of Use act as a safeguard against misuse of their services.

    Example: A Pakistani online education platform may prohibit sharing of course content without permission, protecting their intellectual property.

7.1.2 Common Clauses and Conditions​

Terms of Use usually contain several common clauses designed to protect both the provider and the user:

ClauseDescriptionExample
User ObligationsWhat is expected from the userRide-sharing apps like Careem require a valid phone number and prohibit non-transportation misuse
Limitations of LiabilityLimits provider's liability if something goes wrongOnline banking Terms of Use limit liability for app downtime
Privacy and Data UseHow the company collects, uses, and protects user dataWhatsApp outlines how messages are stored and protected
Intellectual Property RightsProtects content owned by the providerPakistani news websites prohibit copying and distributing articles without permission
Termination of ServiceConditions under which provider can terminate accessFacebook can suspend accounts that violate community standards

Figure 7.1: Common clauses found in Terms of Use agreements

7.1.3 Legal Ramifications​

Violating the Terms of Use can lead to serious legal consequences — users can be fined, sued, or banned from using the service.

Example: Using software without a proper license may result in legal action for software piracy. Companies using unlicensed software face legal risks and cybersecurity threats.

7.1.4 Ethical Considerations​

Ethical considerations in Terms of Use include fairness, transparency, and respect for user rights. Companies must ensure their terms are clear, straightforward, and not overly complex or deceptive.

Example: When users sign up for an online course in Pakistan, the terms should clearly specify whether a certificate will be awarded, how personal data will be handled, and whether there are additional fees.

7.1.5 Personal Rights​

Personal rights within the Terms of Use include the right to privacy, the right to be informed, and the right to withdraw consent.

Example: Under Pakistan's Personal Data Protection Bill, users have the right to access their personal data and request corrections or deletions. When using local e-commerce websites, users have the right to know how their personal information (addresses, phone numbers) will be utilized.

Did You Know? Some online platforms frequently update their Terms of Use. It's a good practice to review them periodically to stay informed about any changes.

7.2 Privacy and Security Threats​

In today's digital world, privacy and security threats are major concerns for individuals and organizations alike. These threats have the potential to compromise personal information, financial data, and overall online security.

7.2.1 Types of Security Threats​

Figure 7.2: Five primary categories of online security threats

7.2.1.1 Spam​

Spam refers to unwanted messages received via email or phone.

Example: You may receive numerous text messages from unknown numbers offering products or services you did not request.

7.2.1.2 Spyware​

Spyware is harmful software that secretly monitors your activities on your computer or mobile phone.

Example: A free app downloaded from an unreliable source may install spyware on your device, secretly monitoring websites you visit or information you enter.

7.2.1.3 Cookies​

Cookies are small files that websites place on your device when you visit them. They help websites remember login details and personal preferences — but can also track your online activities.

Example: When you visit an online shopping site, cookies save your login information. However, they also track products you view to show related advertisements.

7.2.1.4 Phishing​

Phishing is a scam where someone pretends to be a trustworthy organization to trick you into giving away personal information.

Example: You may receive an email that looks like it comes from your bank, asking you to click a link and enter your account details. The email is fake — if you follow the instructions, your money could be at risk.

Did You Know? Over 90% of phishing attacks are carried out through email. Always verify the source before clicking any links or providing personal information.

7.2.1.5 Pharming​

Pharming is a sophisticated technique where users are redirected to a fake website without their knowledge.

Example: You type in the URL for your bank's website, but due to a pharming attack, you are sent to a counterfeit site that looks just like the real one. If you enter your login information, the scammer collects your details.

7.2.2 Security Threat Prevention Techniques​

ThreatPrevention Technique
SpamSpam filters automatically move unwanted emails to a separate folder
SpywareAntivirus software scans files for hidden threats before you open them
CookiesCookie management — keep trusted site cookies, delete cookies from unfamiliar sites
PhishingRecognize fake emails; never click suspicious links or provide personal information
PharmingAlways verify the URL is correct and look for "https://" and a padlock symbol

7.2.2.1 Spam Filters​

Spam filters help keep unwanted emails out of your inbox by automatically moving them to a separate folder or deleting them.

7.2.2.2 Antivirus Software​

Antivirus software protects your computer from harmful programs like spyware. If you download a file from the internet, antivirus software checks it for hidden threats and alerts you if something is harmful.

7.2.2.3 Cookie Management​

Managing cookies means deciding which cookies to keep and which to delete. Keep cookies from trusted sites for convenience; delete cookies from unfamiliar sites to protect your privacy.

7.2.2.4 Recognizing Phishing​

Identifying phishing emails involves looking for signs of fakery — unusual sender addresses, urgent language, requests for personal information — and avoiding clicking any links or providing any information.

7.2.2.5 Guarding Against Pharming​

Always check that a website's address is correct and secure. Look for "https://" and a padlock symbol in the browser's address bar before entering any sensitive information.

Tidbits: Effective online security involves using multiple strategies like spam filters, antivirus software, and careful cookie management. Combining different techniques provides better protection than relying on any single one.

7.3 The Digital Divide and Its Impacts​

The digital divide refers to the gap between individuals, communities, and regions that have access to modern Information and Communication Technology (ICT) and those that do not. This divide leads to unequal opportunities in education, employment, healthcare, and social participation.

Figure 7.3: Key causes of the digital divide and its societal impacts

7.3.1 Causes of the Digital Divide​

CauseDescription
Economic BarriersMany individuals and families cannot afford computers, smartphones, or broadband internet — particularly evident in developing countries and low-income areas
Geographical BarriersAccess to ICT varies significantly between urban and rural areas; remote areas often lack internet infrastructure
Educational BarriersLack of digital literacy means people may not know how to use technology effectively even when it is available
Social BarriersAge, gender, and disability can affect access — older adults may struggle with new technologies; women in some regions face cultural barriers

7.3.2 Impacts of the Digital Divide​

7.3.2.1 Educational Inequality​

Access to the internet and digital tools is increasingly essential for education. Students without access are at a significant disadvantage.

Example: During COVID-19, when schools in Pakistan shifted to online learning, students from low-income families or rural areas struggled due to lack of internet access or devices — widening the educational gap between socio-economic groups.

7.3.2.2 Economic Disparities​

Many jobs require at least basic digital skills. Those without access to technology may find it challenging to secure employment.

Example: In Pakistan, access to online job portals and digital banking services is often limited to urban areas, further widening the economic gap between urban and rural populations.

7.3.2.3 Social and Civic Participation​

Those without internet access are often excluded from social media, online news, and digital government services — limiting their participation in democratic processes.

Example: In Pakistan, e-government services are more readily available in cities, leaving rural populations with limited means to access social services or participate in governmental processes.

7.3.2.4 Health Disparities​

Digital platforms provide valuable resources for health education, telemedicine, and appointment scheduling. Rural communities with limited internet access miss out.

Example: Rural communities in Pakistan may struggle to find reliable health information online or participate in telemedicine consultations.

7.3.2.5 Digital Literacy Gap​

Individuals who do not have access to technology may not develop essential digital skills, further reinforcing the divide.

Example: People in Pakistan who lack access to computers and the internet may miss out on opportunities to learn and practice digital skills.

7.3.3 Bridging the Digital Divide​

Bridging the digital divide means making sure everyone has equal access to technology and the internet:

  1. Government Initiatives — Build infrastructure for internet access in underserved areas
  2. Educational Programs — Schools and community centers can teach digital literacy
  3. Public-Private Partnerships — Government, businesses, and nonprofits working together can make technology more affordable

Did You Know? The Government of Pakistan has launched several initiatives to increase internet access in rural areas, including the introduction of mobile broadband services in remote regions.

Class Activity: Think about your own community. Are there people who may not have access to the internet or digital devices? Write a short paragraph on how your school or community can help bridge the digital divide.

7.4 Computing's Impact on Individuals and Society​

Computing systems have revolutionized the way we live, work, and communicate — bringing both positive and negative changes to society.

7.4.1 Positive Impacts​

  • Increased Access to Information: The internet allows people to access vast amounts of information on nearly any topic, breaking down barriers to education and knowledge.

    Example: Platforms like Coursera and edX allow students from remote areas to take courses from prestigious universities worldwide.

  • Improved Communication: Social media platforms like Facebook and X (Twitter) facilitate personal connections and enable social movements.

    Example: In Pakistan, social media played a crucial role in raising awareness about the 2014 Peshawar school attack, mobilizing support and solidarity.

  • Economic Growth: Computing has created new industries and job opportunities. E-commerce platforms like Daraz have transformed traditional business models, allowing small businesses to reach customers nationwide and internationally.

7.4.2 Negative Impacts​

  • Digital Divide: A significant portion of the population lacks access to modern computing technologies. In Pakistan, the urban-rural divide leads to unequal opportunities in education and employment.

  • Misinformation and Fake News: Can lead to social unrest.

    Example: During COVID-19, misinformation about the virus and vaccines spread rapidly on social media, causing confusion and fear — highlighting the need for digital literacy.

  • Privacy Concerns: Increased use of computing systems leads to vast collection of personal data. Without proper regulations, this data can be misused.

    Example: In Pakistan, leakage of citizens' data from NADRA underscores the importance of robust data protection measures.

Did You Know? "Internet in a Box" projects provide offline educational resources. In Pakistan, such initiatives have brought digital learning to remote areas without internet access.

7.5 Digital Citizenship and Ethical Considerations​

Digital citizenship involves understanding how to behave responsibly and ethically when using digital technologies and the internet. It encompasses behaviors and practices that help ensure a positive and secure online experience for everyone.

Figure 7.4: The key pillars of responsible digital citizenship

Example: In Pakistan, students should learn to communicate respectfully in online forums and social media — avoiding bullying, not spreading false information, and understanding the importance of digital privacy.

7.5.1 Responsible Digital Behavior​

Responsible digital behavior means using technology and the internet in ways that are respectful and safe:

  1. Using Strong Passwords — Create passwords that are difficult for others to guess. Use a combination of letters, numbers, and symbols (e.g., not "password123").
  2. Avoiding Phishing Scams — Do not click on suspicious links or open emails from unknown sources asking for personal information.

7.5.2 Ethical Use of Information​

Ethical use of information involves handling data and content in a fair and lawful manner.

7.5.2.1 Responsible Data Sharing​

Share personal or sensitive information only when absolutely necessary and only with trusted entities.

Example: When applying for a school scholarship online in Pakistan, students should provide personal details only on secure and verified websites.

7.5.2.2 Ethical Issues​

IssueDescription
Respecting CopyrightDo not copy or use someone else's work without permission. When writing a research paper, do not copy text from online articles without citing the source.
Avoiding PlagiarismProperly attribute ideas and information to their original authors. Mention the source of any quotes or ideas used in assignments.

7.5.3 Cybersecurity Awareness​

Cybersecurity awareness involves understanding and implementing practices to protect oneself from online threats.

Did You Know? A Cyber Crime can be reported at NR3C-FIA available at https://nr3c.gov.pk

7.5.4 Collaborative Problem Solving​

Collaborative problem solving involves working together with others and with technology to address challenges.

7.5.4.1 Human-Machine Collaboration​

ApproachDescription
Using ToolsLeverage software and digital tools to assist in tasks (e.g., spreadsheet software to analyze data for a project)
Combining SkillsIntegrate human judgment with machine capabilities — use data analysis tools to interpret complex datasets while applying human reasoning to make decisions

7.5.4.2 Teamwork and Knowledge Sharing​

  • Group Projects: Work together on assignments, each contributing expertise
  • Sharing Insights: Exchange ideas and knowledge with peers in study groups or discussions to broaden understanding

7.5.5 Creating Accessible Digital Content​

Creating accessible digital content means designing and producing content that everyone can use, including people with disabilities.

7.5.5.1 Design for Accessibility​

  • Readable Fonts: Use clear, large fonts like Arial or Times New Roman — avoid overly decorative styles
  • Alternative Text for Images: Provide descriptions for images so visually impaired users can understand the content

7.5.5.2 User Feedback​

  • Surveys and Forms: Collect feedback on digital content and make improvements based on responses
  • Direct Communication: Engage with users to understand their needs and preferences

Note: In 2020, Pakistan introduced the Personal Data Protection Bill, which aims to protect citizens' data and ensure their privacy. This bill requires organizations to obtain explicit consent before collecting personal data and mandates secure storage of such data.
